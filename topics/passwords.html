Passwords & Authentication

Why long passwords beat complex ones, and how multi-factor authentication adds a second layer of protection.

Length beats complexity

A long passphrase made of several random words (like correct-horse-battery-staple) is harder for an attacker to guess or crack than a short password stuffed with symbols, and it's easier for a person to remember. Current guidance from NIST favors length over forced complexity rules like "must include a symbol."

Multi-factor authentication (MFA)

MFA asks for a second proof of identity beyond your password, so a stolen password alone isn't enough to get into your account. The three common factors are:

Authentication factors
Factor Example Why it helps
Something you know Password or PIN The baseline factor most accounts already use
Something you have Phone app code, security key An attacker also needs your physical device
Something you are Fingerprint, face scan Much harder to steal or copy remotely

Quick habits

  • Use a password manager so every account gets a unique password.
  • Turn on MFA anywhere it's offered, especially email and banking.
  • Never reuse the same password across multiple sites.
